# Critical Thinking Prompts

Prompts that challenge assumptions, surface blind spots, and sharpen reasoning.

---

## Socratic Method — 7 Rules as Prompts

Source: Natan Mohart infographic

Apply Socrates' 7 critical thinking rules to any argument, claim, or idea.

### 1. Define Terms
```
What do you mean by [KEY TERM]? Give me a precise definition before we proceed.
```
**Rule:** Most debates fail because people don't agree on definitions.

### 2. Question Assumptions
```
Why do you believe [CLAIM]? What assumptions are hiding underneath this?
```
**Rule:** Every claim hides assumptions. Expose them — and you expose the strength or weakness of the idea.

### 3. Demand Evidence
```
Can you give me a concrete example of [CLAIM]? What data or case study supports this?
```
**Rule:** Facts and examples transform vague opinions into grounded knowledge.

### 4. Test Consequences
```
What happens if we act on [IDEA]? What are the second and third-order consequences?
```
**Rule:** Strong ideas survive a reality check. This question reveals hidden risks.

### 5. Spot Contradictions
```
Does [CLAIM A] conflict with [CLAIM B]? Where does the logic break down?
```
**Rule:** Contradictions show where logic breaks. Consistency is the test of truth.

### 6. Doubt the Obvious
```
What if [WIDELY ACCEPTED BELIEF] isn't true? What evidence would challenge it?
```
**Rule:** The most dangerous beliefs are the ones no one questions. Progress starts with doubt.

### 7. Consider Alternatives
```
What would the opposite view say? What's the strongest case for the other side?
```
**Rule:** Examining other perspectives prevents blind spots and improves decisions.

## The Assumption Detector

When you're convinced about something and want a reality check:

```
I believe [YOUR BELIEF]. What hidden assumptions am I making? What evidence might contradict this?
```

**When to use:** Before committing to a decision you feel certain about.
**Why it works:** Surfaces beliefs you accepted without evidence before they cause expensive mistakes.

---

## The Devil's Advocate

When you're in love with your own idea:

```
I'm planning to [YOUR IDEA]. If you were trying to convince me this is a terrible idea, what would be your most compelling arguments?
```

**When to use:** Before launching, investing, or committing to something you're excited about.
**Why it works:** Forces the model to argue against you instead of validating you. Reveals fatal flaws while you can still change course.

---

## The Ripple Effect Analyzer

Before making a big change:

```
I'm thinking about [POTENTIAL DECISION]. Beyond the obvious first-order effects, what might be the unexpected second and third-order consequences?
```

**When to use:** Career moves, product decisions, policy changes, major purchases.
**Why it works:** Most analysis stops at the obvious outcome. This prompt forces the model to think two steps ahead — where the real surprises live.

---

## The Blind Spot Illuminator

When facing a persistent problem you can't solve:

```
I keep experiencing [PROBLEM] despite [YOUR SOLUTION ATTEMPTS]. What factors might I be overlooking?
```

**When to use:** Recurring problems, persistent underperformance, team issues that won't resolve.
**Why it works:** Asks the model to look outside your existing frame — exactly where blind spots live.

## Root Cause Analysis — F.O.C.U.S. Framework

Source: Justin Mecham infographic

```
Analyze this problem using the F.O.C.U.S. framework:

Problem: [DESCRIBE THE PROBLEM]

F — Focus on the actual problem (not symptoms)
O — Organize the data you need
C — Create a list of possible causes
U — Understand the underlying systems
S — Solve using targeted actions

Also apply the 5 Whys: Ask "why?" five times in sequence to uncover the root cause, not the surface symptom.

Avoid these common mistakes:
- Jumping to conclusions without enough data
- Treating symptoms instead of finding the real cause
- Blaming individuals instead of fixing system flaws
```
